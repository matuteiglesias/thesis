Explaining pLQ by Growth Distributions

Understanding the Probability of Surpassing the LQ=1LQ = 1LQ=1 Threshold

To translate the characterization of jumps in x1x_1x1​, x2x_2x2​, and thus log⁡(LQ)\log(LQ)log(LQ) into the probability of surpassing the LQ=1LQ = 1LQ=1 threshold (denoted as pLQ), we need to focus on explaining pLQ through growth distributions. Our task is to count all cases where jumps result in log⁡(LQ)>1\log(LQ) > 1log(LQ)>1.

Integrating Growth Distributions​

This involves integrating the growth distributions that describe the volatility of x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​. For any initial point x1x_1x1​, x2x_2x2​, consider combinations of jumps in both x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ that might result in exceeding the threshold in the next timestep. If an observation is below the LQ=1LQ = 1LQ=1 threshold, it can surpass it by either x2x_2x2​ shrinking enough or x1x_1x1​ growing sufficiently. These represent the pure x1x_1x1​, x2x_2x2​ directions of the oblique axes in the plot (see Figure: Coordinates). Intermediate combinations of changes in x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​, if intense enough, may also achieve this. The general accounting for these situations is done via integration in the equation for pLQ.

Assuming the growth rate distribution is separable, it can be expressed as a product of two one-dimensional marginal growth distributions: G0(Δx2,Δx1)=gx2(Δx2)gx1(Δx1)G_{0} (\Delta x_2, \Delta x_1) = g_{x_2} (\Delta x_2) g_{x_1} (\Delta x_1)G0​(Δx2​,Δx1​)=gx2​​(Δx2​)gx1​​(Δx1​). This is integrated numerically in the x1>x2x_1 > x_2x1​>x2​ region.

Numerical Integration Procedure​

For the numerical integration of these 2D growth rate probabilities, the following procedure is applied:

  • Binning Observations: Observations are binned into intervals with centers {x1}\{x_1\}{x1​}. By comparing observations at consecutive time periods, histograms of Δx1\Delta x_1Δx1​ are observed for each bin. These histograms, normalized by bin population, estimate the assumed gx1(Δx1)g_{x_1} (\Delta x_1)gx1​​(Δx1​).
  • Creating Interpolators: A 2D continuous interpolator gx1(Δx1)g_{x_1} (\Delta x_1)gx1​​(Δx1​) is created to estimate the chances of x1x_1x1​ becoming x1+Δx1x_1 + \Delta x_1x1​+Δx1​ after one time period. The same procedure is applied to the x2x_2x2​ variable.
  • Estimating G0G_0G0​: G0G_0G0​ is estimated as gx2gx1g_{x_2} g_{x_1}gx2​​gx1​​ for the chances of jumping in the 2D LQ plane.

The numerical integration is performed by evaluating growth rate interpolators on a fine grid covering a large rectangle about point x=(x2,x1)\textbf{x} = (x_2, x_1)x=(x2​,x1​) and storing it in a 2D numpy array G. The condition x1>x2x_1 > x_2x1​>x2​ is stored in another array C of the same shape. The integration is computed as (G * C).sum() / G.sum().

Implications of Volatility Decay​

The decay of volatility with size (see Figure: Volatility Decay) implies that larger observations and those from larger countries and products are less volatile. This feature results in a lower likelihood for a large observation s1s_{1}s1​ to surpass LQ=1LQ = 1LQ=1, compared to a smaller observation s2s_{2}s2​, given they start at the same LQ<1LQ < 1LQ<1 level.

Figure: Growth Rate Model illustrates the model of uncorrelated growth rates in the variables x1=log⁡(scp)x_1 = \log(s_{cp})x1​=log(scp​) and x2=log⁡(ScSp/SW)x_2 = \log(S_c S_p / S_W)x2​=log(Sc​Sp​/SW​). The ellipses depict the standard deviation of these variables. Changes in the transition width of pLQ can be qualitatively explained by the dependence of the moments std(x1)std(x_1)std(x1​) and std(x2)std(x_2)std(x2​) on x1x_1x1​, x2x_2x2​.

Validating the Model​

To validate this model for reconstructing pLQ, a comparison is made with level curves of pLQ from the knn estimator. A qualitative match is observed in the patterns of widening of the effective LQ metric with decreasing x1,x2x_1, x_2x1​,x2​ values. The transition x1=x2=(x1+x2)/2x_1 = x_2 = (x_1 + x_2)/2x1​=x2​=(x1​+x2​)/2 and size effects relevant to the LQ=1LQ = 1LQ=1 level can be evaluated by measuring std(Δlog⁡(scp))std(\Delta \log(s_{cp}))std(Δlog(scp​)) and std(Δlog⁡(ScSp/SW))std(\Delta \log(S_c S_p / S_W))std(Δlog(Sc​Sp​/SW​)) along this line.

Generalizing the paths suggested here may allow further exercises where the volatility in log⁡(LQ)\log(LQ)log(LQ) is explained by volatilities in all four sizes log⁡(scp)\log(s_{cp})log(scp​), log⁡(Sc)\log(S_c)log(Sc​), log⁡(Sp)\log(S_p)log(Sp​), and log⁡(SW)\log(S_W)log(SW​) separately. The formal tools can be extended to cover such settings, although the schemes may need to be adapted for four variables instead of two.
