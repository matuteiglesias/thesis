Non-Linearities​

Understanding Nonlinearities in Economic Fluctuations

Fluctuations in the level of agents, when expressed on a logarithmic scale, imply that nominal contributions from different agents are represented by an exponential function. In this context, nonlinearities refer to the addition of levels along an exponential curve. Let's delve into the general properties of a logarithmic transformation.

Logarithmic Transformations​

Consider a scenario where 100 extra EUR sold by a firm directly translates to aggregate exports. Although this amount seems 'insignificant' compared to total exports, which can exceed 100 billion EUR, this insignificance can be formalized by examining the ratio between these levels. For instance, the ratio is 1.0000001, indicating that aggregate exports remain largely unchanged after the 100 EUR addition. This can be expressed as St/S0=(109+102)/109=1+10−7S_t / S_0 = (10^9 + 10^2)/ 10^9 = 1 + 10^{-7}St​/S0​=(109+102)/109=1+10−7, a useful transformation for abstracting changes from the nominal scale.

Alternatively, considering equal multiplicative changes to XXX as equally important (rather than their additive magnitude) is achieved by evaluating additive changes of a logarithmic transformation of XXX, denoted log⁡(X)\log(X)log(X).

Taylor Series Approximation​

For practical purposes, we can treat log⁡(z):R→R\log(z): \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}log(z):R→R as a nonlinear function approximated by a Taylor series. The base is 10, unless otherwise stated. Its Taylor series about z0z_0z0​ is:

where the factor ln⁡(10)≈2.3026\ln(10) \approx 2.3026ln(10)≈2.3026 is the natural logarithm of 10. This factor appears repeatedly due to our use of base 10 instead of natural logarithms, aiding in the interpretation of log levels (e.g., 6 = 1 million EUR, 9 = 1 billion EUR).

First Order Approximation​

The expansion of log⁡(z)\log(z)log(z) (base 10) at z0=1z_0 = 1z0​=1 up to the first order is:

(z−1)≈ln⁡(10)log⁡(z)(z - 1) \approx \ln(10) \log(z)(z−1)≈ln(10)log(z)

This relation becomes practical if we identify z=Xt/Xˉ≈1z = X_t / \bar X \approx 1z=Xt​/Xˉ≈1, leading to:

Xt−XˉXˉ≈ln⁡(10)log⁡(XtXˉ)⇒ΔXXˉ≈ln⁡(10)Δ(log⁡(X))\frac{X_t - \bar X}{\bar X} \approx \ln(10) \log\left(\frac{X_t}{\bar X} \right) \Rightarrow \frac{\Delta X}{\bar X} \approx \ln(10) \Delta (\log(X))XˉXt​−Xˉ​≈ln(10)log(XˉXt​​)⇒XˉΔX​≈ln(10)Δ(log(X))

This equation highlights the connection between small nominal deviations from the stationary level Xˉ\bar XXˉ, percentage change factors, and logarithmic fluctuation. It's crucial to recognize when this equation does not apply, which is fairly often.

Second Order Approximation​

Approximating the log curve at z0=1z_0 = 1z0​=1 to the second order gives:

log⁡(z) ln⁡(10)≈(z−1)−12(z−1)2\log(z)\ \ln(10) \approx (z - 1) - \frac{1}{2}(z - 1)^{2}log(z) ln(10)≈(z−1)−21​(z−1)2

Thus, the rough condition for the first order approximation to be valid requires:

(ΔX/Xˉ)22≪1\frac{({\Delta X}/{\bar X})^2}{2} \ll 12(ΔX/Xˉ)2​≪1

The second order error term is about 0.005 for deviations of 10% and 0.1 for deviations of 50%. This means that for time series fluctuating around 10% or less, it is safe to use the first order approximation, but not for more volatile series.

St/Sˉ=10FtS_t / \bar S = 10^{F_t}St​/Sˉ=10Ft​
